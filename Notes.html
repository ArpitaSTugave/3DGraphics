The ideal focus is a mental aberration

Treating all optics as an 'aberration' from an approximate ideal developed for telescopes
(where rays stay very close to the axis, in direction as well as distance) 
is like treating all biology in terms of how far each animal deviates from being a unicorn.

The standard target in creating an optical image of a scene with many separate points
is to bring light from each separate point to its own separate focus,
in a useful place like a camera film or a sensor array.

This is much more difficult than the textbooks make it sound.
For instance, they point out that parallel rays reflected by a spherical mirror
do not all pass through a common point (Fig. __).
This they call 'aberration'
They then show that reflections of rays parallel to the axis of a parabolic mirror
do pass through a common point (Fig. __).
This they consider perfection.

[insert interactive images with each kind of mirror:  two figures.
Captions mention '2d cross-section'.]

Problem solved!   
Yes?

The contrast is endlessly repeated around books and the web,
giving the impression that the spherical mirror is a horrible mess,
while the parabolic mirror is perfection.
The discussion usually avoids looking at what is involved in forming an image,
focussing light from different points at different points on (for instance) a camera film.
For very distant objects we can consider the arriving rays as parallel to one another,
but if they are parallel to the axis of the mirror they come from one distant point.
An image involves a lot of distant points, all at different angles to that axis.
So what happens for the off-axis source points?

In the figures above, you can drag the source and see.
Dragging the yellow patch on the left keeps the rays parallel: 
drag to the right, and we see rays coming from a finite distance.

For the spherical mirror, the pattern the rays make stays much the same —
moving a source at infinity just rotates the pattern around the sphere's centre.
Even coming to a finite distance just warps it a bit.

For the parabolic mirror, the focus explodes into something quite different.
The spherical mirror creates a stable pattern:
the parabolic mirror creates a mess, waiting to happen.

For small angles the mess is small.
For very small angles it is small enough that we can pretend it is a point.
For a narrow field of view, that pretence works … more or less …
but the language of 'aberration' does not help us think about its failures.

The unicorn's horn comes to an exact sharp point,
but unicorns are hard to find, and even in myth they are solitary.
A perfect focus is almost as hard to find, and equally solitary.
Optics design that depends on creating a whole sheet of perfect focus points
is like planning in terms of whole herds of unicorns.
Perhaps we can do better, by being more realistic about light.

Another standard example, useful in brainwashing students
to think of the geometrically perfect focus as a real thing,
involves reflections from an elliptical mirror.
Light from one ellipse-focus bounces exactly to the other,
giving an exact ray-optics focus, all rays through one point. 
Googling 'Ellipse Reflection Property' gives a third of a million hits, and a sea of images.
(You probably met it in your teens, if you're the kind of person to be reading this.)
As the version below opens, the rays from the right-hand ellipse-focus
all pass through the left-hand one, forming a theoretically perfect light-focus.
Before you start to drag the flashing source around:
what do you think that this light-focus will do?

Fig. 1: There is nothing special about the proportions of this ellipse: 
you can change it by dragging the handle at the top.

Drag the source off the right ellipse-focus point (green dot), just a little,
and the light-focus at the left doesn't just move off the left ellipse-focus.
It explodes into a caustic curve, with cusps that move violently as you drag.

The light-focus does not merely move, like the pattern in [fig spherical mirror] .
It disintegrates, like the point focus in [parabolic mirror figure].

Setting the stage, how do we describe light? 

Rays and waves
Rays are a geometric approximation to energy transmission by waves 
(of light, of ultrasound, of water height).
They are a close and useful fit, as long as the wavelength
is short compared to the distances of physical interest.
The fine detail of the caustics we discuss does demand wave theory,
but ray geometry reveals much about 'aberration' and image formation.

Here is a simple example of robust focussing, in the ray view:
a close-up view of [spherical mirror figure], with the reflecting circle complete,
and the source starting at a point on the circle.

Fig. 3: Reflections from a circular mirror.

Rays from the blinking point reflect off the circle.
They create bright 'fold' curves (obviously named)
and a 'cusp' (from Latin cuspis — a point, spear, or pointed end).
(The drawing stops all the rays just before their second bounce, for clarity.)
Drag the blinking point, and the cusp moves smoothly, as do the folds leading from it.

Light following those rays shows brighter along the folds, as we see with real light:


Fig. 4: Light source just inside a cylindrical mirror.

At folds and cusps, the Sun's effect is both brighter and hotter,
giving the name caustic, or 'burning'.
One limitation of ray theory is in calculating intensities there:
suppose that between any two neighbouring rays, the same energy flows outward.
The initial intensity drop is obvious as the rays separate:
the visual density of lines gives a good qualitative sense of it.
As they re-concentrate after reflection, neighbours get closer, visual density rises.
This remains a good cue to real physical intensity, until neighbours get close to crossing.
The ray approximation gives an energy density that approaches infinity.
With a small non-zero wavelength the intensity remains bounded,
and reaches its maximum near — but not exactly at — the cusped curve.
Real intensities are important for many applications, but 'oscillatory integrals'
are beyond our scope here: see Nye and its references for more.
(Chapters 1–4  are at the ray level used here; 5 and 6 go to wavelength scale; 
7–11 go larger, to the statistics of caustic patterns; the last chapters treat polarisation.)
Here, we just take visible concentration at a point 
`q`
 
among a family
`R`
of rays 
as a qualitative sign that much of the energy of 
`R`
is arriving near 
`q`

We said above "Drag the blinking point, and the cusp moves smoothly".
Before we can say 'it' moves, we have to agree 'it' persists: it does.
Wherever we drag the source, off the circle centre, there is a corresponding cusp point,
around which some 20th Century mathematics shows that a smooth coordinate transformation
gives an exact match to the original geometry.   
The cusp is stable, and moves without jumps,
unlike the explosions for a parabolic or elliptical mirror.
Focus explosion is typical, with lenses as well as mirrors,
and makes nonsense of standard optics ideas like Petzval field curvature.
One account of this idea is a Wikipedia article,
which has the typical drawing in Fig. 5.

Fig. 5: A typical 'field curvature' diagram.
.

 Fig. 5 suggests/assumes two unlikely things.

The first unlikely thing is that all the red rays, arriving parallel to the axis,
go through a single 'perfect focus' point.
This is untrue if the lens faces are parts of spheres
(the easiest kind to grind, by an inherently precise process),
as Kepler understood well:

Fig. 6: In 1611, Kepler drew the failure of a lens to deflect parallel rays through a single point.

(Kepler gave the word focus — in Latin, fireplace — its current meaning, 
from the heat intensity when you concentrate sunlight.
He analysed telescopes, designed them and made them.)
The pattern the rays make is a cusp, as with the spherical mirror.
(Cusps are not merely typical, in the ordinary-English sense(s).
They are generic in a mathematically provable sense.
We won't cover here the formal definition or proof,
but we hope to give a sense of why cusps are so common.)

If there is no single intersection point, even for axis-parallel rays,
how can 'that point' move along the curve in Fig. 5, 
as the ray direction swings from the red to the blue?
If there is no curve of points it moves along, 
how can the curve have curvature?
Only in dodgy approximations.

It is possible to compute a lens shape (for a chosen refractive index)
for which rays arriving parallel to the axis do bend to go through the exact same point.
It is even possible to make one, though not with the easier sphere-grinding techniques.
(This makes it more costly, and gives it the nonsensical industry name aspheric lens.
True, it's not spherical: also true, you are not an elephant.
Should your doctor's textbooks be on non-elephant biology?)  
Fig. 7 shows an example: if you change the refractive index
or the desired focal length, your computer recomputes the lens shape.
In the short-wavelength limit, all the light goes through one point…
as long it arrives parallel to the axis. 
For simplicity, stay in 2D, where the lens meets air in a curve, not a surface.

Fig. 7: A lens that makes axis-parallel rays converge exactly to a point focus.

Move your cursor along the left edge of Fig. 7,
and you drag the parallel beam to arrive from above or below the axis.

Move your cursor inward, and you have a single point that the rays come from.

The moment this source is no longer the 'ideal' source for the design, 
meaning 'at infinity' and on the axis (like [fig parabolic mirror] until you drag), 
or like the un-dragged source in Fig. 1, 
the 'perfect focus' of arriving rays explodes.
With this narrow-beam source, it doesn't widen much,
but it grows fast into a long caustic curve.
Which point on that curve should be called 'the focus',
and used to define the Petzval surface?
Standard references — you can't get much more standard than SPIE,
the International Society for Optics and Photonics — say things like
"A perfect image is formed on a curved surface,"
but a perfect image is made of perfect focus points,
and a perfect focus occurs for at most one ray-origin point.
Each point in a scene lies in its own direction from the camera,
different from all others, and often at different distances.
Change from the single point source (perhaps at infinity)
that the lens was designed for, and 'perfection' disappears.
Petzval curvature is an approximation to the curvature
of a surface that does not actually exist.

It turns out useful, sometimes, to pretend that the surface does exist:
but optics students get no guidance in when the pretence will cause trouble.

On the other hand, consider a regular spherical lens.
This never creates a perfect focus, as Kepler knew.
The outer rays meet earlier, but they don't
"produce many focal points on the principal axis before the actual focus",
as suggested in Wikipedia and also in Fig. 8:

Fig. 8: A typical misleading diagram. from a telescope site. 
There is nothing focal about the left point marked. 


As  Fig. 9 shows, the bright 'focussing' is along a cusped curve,
a caustic, very like the one in Fig. 3.
There is a particularly bright point at the cusp tip,
though only one ray goes exactly through it.


Fig. 9: A spherical lens, flat on one side for simplicity.
Incident rays become axis-parallel if you drag the source left.

Like Fig. 3, and unlike  [parabolic mirror figure] and Fig. 1,
this pattern does not disintegrate as the source moves.
If we move the source 'at infinity' by dragging along the left of the figure,
changing the direction of the parallel beam, the cusp point does trace out a curve:
not all the light from each source direction goes through the corresponding point,
but each source has a point where its light passes most densely.
These points trace out a well-defined Petzval-ish curve
(and in 3D a surface), but only because they are not perfect foci.
They are structurally stable, meaning that if we perturb anything
(the direction, the parallelism, the lens shape, …) a small amount,
the caustic curve continues to exist.  Its bright cusp tip continues to exist,
and the bright tip moves smoothly as a function of the perturbation.
If we move the source at a fixed finite distance (try it!),
the bright tip traces a different curve, with its own curvature.

The mathematics to justify these statements, and calculate with them,
is only half a century old, so it is known to optical physicists
but has not reached optical engineers or ophthalmologists.
An introduction in general is Catastrophe Theory and Its Applications,
but before applying that theory here, it helps to look closer at light ray basics.


The variational nature of rays

Fermat’s half-truth

"The actual path between two points taken by a beam of light
is the one which is traversed in the least time."

This early form of Fermat's Principle is still common in schoolbooks,
and in awed discussions about the mystical economy of nature.

It is only half true.

It is true that
____ If some path is traversed in less time than any other,
____  a beam of light will take it.
It is false that 
____ If a path is taken by light,
____  that path is traversed in the least time.

Figures 10 and 11 are good examples of the true version:
light does follow the black paths, wherever you put `p` and `q`.
To see the problem with the old version, we return to circle reflections, 
as in Fig. 3, but add a choice of arrival point `q`.

Fig. 12 replaces the straight reflector `R` in Fig. 11 by a circle.
The green lines go straight from `p` to any point `r` on `R`, then from `r` to `q`. 
As in Fig. 11, time is proportional to length, and marked by moving dashes.
At right we graph the length as a function of `r`, from the rightmost point (0°)
around anticlockwise to the leftmost (180°) and back to (360°=0°).

In the starting position of Fig. 12 , the graph has one minimum and one maximum.
The minimum corresponds to a reflection at lower right,
the maximum corresponds to a reflection at upper left,
and both are realised as actual rays, shown black.
Each satisfies the reflection law, making equal angles to the normal
(which here points to the centre of `R`) at its particular `r`.

The longest green path is not actually maximal among all possible paths.
Those would allow all sorts of wiggles, and hence longer ways from `p` to `r` to `q`.
However, that path is critical, in the sense that any nearby path
gives 'to first order' the same arrival time.
Proper mathematical treatment of that involves the Calculus of Variations,
which we will skip here, just noticing that where the graph in Fig. 12 has non-zero slope,
the lengths of nearby paths are far more significantly different.
This makes arrival times of anything propagating along those paths significantly different.
If the 'anything' is oscillatory, like quantum wavicles, the arrival phases vary fast,
so the oscillations cancel out in destructive interference.
It is only along critical rays that neighbours interfere constructively,
and photons act as if they follow those rays.

If the las paragraph is baffling, just hold on to experimental fact:  
paths critical for arrival time do get realised as actual light rays.
A minimum is automatically critical, but a critical path need not be minimal.

We are now ready to actually calculate about these rays and caustics.

The key idea  is to look at the slope of the way that length depends on path.


Fig. 23: Path length

Fig. 23 shows not only the path length
`l`

the proportional change in

As high-school calculus teaches, we have 
`dl`/`dr = 0` at the maxima and minima,
so we see these values of `r` as zero crossings of the blue graph `dl`/`dr`.
This becomes quite dramatic as we (for instance) drag q around a cusp in Fig. 23.
Cross one fold and create a new pair of slope-zeroes, and the corresponding rays.
Go on to cross the other, and one of them merges with a different slope-zero,
in mutual annihilation.

However, when `q` is not on a caustic, each ray exists in a stable way, moving with `q`.
We can see that in the ray graphic at left:  the graph of `dl`/`dr` explains why.
Look at a place where it crosses `0` with (itself) non-zero slope,
like the single maximum of `l` that Fig. 23 has before any dragging.
The curve `dl`/`dr` descends from positive values, left of the zero point,
to negative values to the right of it.

Any small change in the function `l`, and hence in `dl`/`dr`,
must leave `dl`/`dr` still crossing from positive values to negative ones,
and hence meeting `0`: there must still be a maximum, 
If the change is due to a change in `q`, there must still be a ray:
an `r` for which 'bounce off  `r`' has maximum length among the green paths.
This `r` does change with `q`, but it moves continuously and differentiably,
by the Inverse Function Theorem (which is usually explained horribly,
in terms of matrices full of partial derivatives, but we can't fix that here).
The ray exists stably until we meet a caustic point,
and both `dl`/`dr` and its own slope are zero together.
Cross the caustic and you can see this happen,
and watch zero-crossings of `dl`/`dr` cancel each other.
Correspondingly, rays vanish.

This local stable existence of rays can be strengthened:
they are stable (up to a smooth change of variables)
even in their mathematical form.
